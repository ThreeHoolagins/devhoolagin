Interviewing: Something's Gotta Give

Preface: I have not interviewed in over 4 months. This is more or less a shower rant, I hope you enjoy.

# Dear Software Recruiters, Interview Designers, and anyone from the business side that would listen

Something has got to change with how we interview and post jobs etc. The interview process is far too exhausting for the health of our industry.

I’ve relatively recently started my position at angi on the security and auth team, and there has been a lot of context gathering that I’ve needed to do. Now this is rather expected, I have moved from a fullstack php/c#/ts webdev role into a backend core systems role, a lot of context is needed for this transition. I really want to do well in my role, and in preparation for taking on this role, I got a book “First 90 Days xxxxx” by xxx” and I wanted to read it and acclimate quickly, read codebases to familiarize myself, go the extra mile to really equip myself for time at my company- those of you more senior reading this may find this naive, and if you do please comment your thoughts. All that in mind, I did not do a lot of these plans. I know I am ambitious beyond my ability but I also can’t help but shake the feeling that I came into my job exhausted from the job search.

My search started at the end of January and I signed papers in late April. I did this, applying and studying outside of my normal work hours, as I was looking to relocate to Colorado from Oklahoma. I applied to roughly 150~ applications, 5~ referrals, contacted everyone I felt I had enough of a connection to ask, got interview invites from 5 companies, and only got past the first interview phase with my current company, which I am very happy with and feel very fortunate for being able to end my job search when I did. I am still getting rejection emails from companies I don’t even remember applying to, and I have 42 saved at time of writing since I decided to start keeping track. At the end of the day, it’s a numbers game, and that game is exhausting.

Now time for my thesis: I think the way we job search in tech (informed from how people hire) exhausts us before we start our new role, leaving us from onboarding our best as an industry.

Now this is more of a personal belief than it is something I can substantiate, so here’s some solutions I have in mind to solve this problem (maybe not problem).

Job postings ought to automatically close.
This can be done in a variety of ways, but once you get past 50-150~ applications, you’re not going to check them all out. Assuming normal rates, you likely will only interview at most 10 candidates from your pool, so this should close once you have a certain amount of applications. This will ensure no one is applying to ghost positions that aren’t open that long. We can do this, we software developers make the platforms all of this is done on.
And please please start closing positions within a reasonable time of opening them. I should not be being given a rejection email 
Job closing needs new language
I for one am exhausted with the amount of “Sowwy mistew, but unfortunately despite how totally amazing and stellar and awesome you are, we just had too many stars like you uwu!” types of rejections that are given, and I even think framing them as rejections is a bad habit of ours. Clean and simply, you should send an email with the phase “Dear X, Thank you for your application to y. We are writing to inform you the position has been filled. You can view other job positions of ours at xyz.com” with marks of “you were not selected due to x” if your company is one to give feedback. The fake empathy towards the job prospect just feels degrading, and ignores the fact that for candidates, your application pool is likely huge, especially since that empathy is in a generated email sent to 500+ candidates. I know there are too many applications, especially automated ones to give quality feedback to everyone. Let’s stop pretending this is how it works for us.
Extra time between jobs should be strongly encouraged, and time ranges should be given to new developers
This is a solution that some of you may have thought of, but money doesn’t grow on trees, and in the job market we are in I don’t want to risk losing a job because I showed hesitance to start during the final stage.
Simply giving people a guide of normal and fair times, and when it would likely be seen as too much without extenuating circumstances would be really nice. Maybe something like 2-4 weeks is expected, 8 weeks is okay with extenuating circumstances (moving, family care, etc)
An even nicer one could be to have some paid time or sign on time to make that money worry a smaller concern to make sure people come in relaxed.
When a recruiter reaches out in regards to your application, you should include a link to the job posting.
This is just a quality of life upgrade for interviewees, but in software you are likely bulk applying. We can ignore this, or we can embrace it and set candidates up for success by linking them back to the position they applied for
