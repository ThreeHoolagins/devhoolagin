Something that’s stuck out to me about being laid off; how much conversation I’ve seen online and how many people have reached out to me regarding my value. I’ve seen many posts of “don’t let layoffs shake you” and “when I was laid off I had mad imposter syndrome” and even someone from angi really sweetly reaching out to let me know that the layoffs don’t say anything about me as a person and that I have great potential. (You know who you are! That was turbo sweet and I appreciate you thinking about me in all of this.)
Strangely, these posts have never resonated with me, and I don’t feel the need for the reassurance, though I cannot stress enough how sweet the sentiment is. I guess to explain this, I gotta tell my story a little bit.
# Acquire to Hire!
My very first and only internship I got from an amazing guy Eric Mathy (add insta/blog) who was on a photojournalism trip writing about (article link here). At the time, I was working at the Center for Sovereign nations as a student leader. We all introduced ourselves, and me mentioning my major was computer engineering caught his attention, and for about a semesters length this guy campaigned for me for an internship position. I’m pretty sure they weren’t even doing an internship program that summer. Respect your mentors!
This leads to my experience that you can see what I did in my resume, I negotiated part time work during my fall semester as a psueo position-psueo extension (I still don’t know what to call it) and ended up changing managers during that fall semester.
And, at the start of December, I was meeting with my manager, and he said he had limited headcount, but if I could learn Data vault 2.0 specs, read a bunch of stuff, and have a good grasp on snowflake and SQL (which I had never used until then) that he would take me on full time in January. That was bananas, and I recognize how lucky I was to have a leader who wanted to support me like that. Thanks Erik! (Funny coincidence for me)
# My first tragedy
Did I mention yet that after my internship I had my first apartment and newlywed wife living with me and the both of us living off my paychecks? Yeah that’s a tough thing when your first job is in 2022 at a start up right when interest rates start ratcheting up. I was employed for 6 months before being laid off.
Oomph.
# That last section was a bummer
So here’s the silver lining if you can call it at this point. I never had an identity crisis about my value at this point, because, well, I wasn’t very good at my job, and everyone else on my team had master’s degrees.
Seriously, and I don’t even feel bad about not being good yet, I was 6 months in to my first job ever in a cost center team, in a state that the company didn’t have a business registered in before my employment, and a number of other things that frankly, I’d pick me too. And I knew my boss didn’t pick me, because he informed me he was informed less than 12 hours in advance. And I was laid off first thing in the morning.
# The CAP
Fast forward a few months and I have a job for Paycom, and fast forward a little over a year into this job and we’ve just wrapped up the extra projects related to GONE, the new flagship project. This had intense stressors on my team, and entire management stack as the CEO was watching this project himself. Spooky.
In the merry month of January, as I’m looking to get my first promotion ever, I’m put on a corrective action plan.
I’ve been hesitant to talk about this due to the possibility of it backfiring on me incase of recruiters reading this, but it’s a core part of not only the story of my career but also my life’s story. But, fully knowing the risks, here we go.
I’ll spare you the details dear reader, but I delayed a project about a week, and was using my pto/sick about as fast as I could accrue it. The first, lead to me, after working through my defensiveness taking significantly more ownership over my work, and the latter was due to some undiagnosed depression/burnout that I had never really realized I had until this period of my life. Turns out, sometimes you’re not lazy, just lacking functional hormones. Or 21, idk fight me.
This period of time, which ended after about 6 weeks of the originally 12 week period, I really developed ownership, fostered a desire for technical ability, and product skills I spoke about in Blog 5: the engineering waltz. Formative time for me as an engineer, as I started listening to Theo and primagen, as you may recognize the callout from blog 1, and read the pragmatic programmer and the software engineers guidebook. I had a lot of time in the car since we had moved to half time in office, and I lived 55 miles away.
# The know it guy 
As GONE obviously caused churn, and people moved up the latter, I quickly became one of the most experienced members left on our team, save one guy who hadn’t moved into management yet.
I got a new team lead, and did my best to be valuable to her, as I had not to long before been in my cap. It was a fresh start. And I did my best to show up the best I could.
The guy happy to answer questions, at any time, in detail
Coming around to the original post, I think knowing my value has come to be oriented around people. Helping more junior peers, being happy to be the guy who explains stuff (in a profession of “too smart for you” developers) I think it’s the thing that’s made me stand out and the thing I’m most prideful of. I love mentoring, and I love to serve. One of these days, I hope to put my skills into writing high quality fast and maintainable software for my local and state government.
# The second layoff
With Angi, I saw the signs, and I talked about it before. Check out the first layoff diaries.
So, how did this not affect my worth? I think the short version is, 1) I had to prove myself both in getting my first job; and in keeping my second, and have been considered a valuable employee by the old director of the Center for Sovereign Nations, and by Patty at Paycom. 2) I’ve been laid off before. I’ve been job searching when I was humbled at paycom, or when I was bored.
I know I can survive being PIP’d/CAP’d/whatever acronym’d. I know layoffs aren’t personal, and above all, I know I got a job about 6 months before the hardest ever job market for juniors tech has ever seen. I got on the last train, and I do my best to be valuable. It makes work feel worth the effort. If I’m not being valuable, than frankly, what the hell am I spending 40 hours a week for years doing? No hate or shade to those who work isn’t a big deal for; but both fighting depression and keeping my job to support the woman I love has meant having to cultivate a care for being useful. It’s honestly survival at this point.
# TLDR; What do I believe makes me valuable
I have a can do attitude and a care for my work. I’ll volunteer to take jira tickets, do my best to learn as much as possible, and I’m always happy to trade 5-10 minutes of my time, where it can save an hour or more of other’s time. Especially with juniors. When you don’t know what to do or how to do it you need someone, and I understand all too well having seniors who handle their pressure from management poorly and it comes off as them being hostile to help you. I never want to be that, and want to be officially in a senior role soon.
Hool
